This is a solid foundation. You have captured the core mechanics of "Volatility Contraction" (similar to Mark      
Minervini’s VCP or Wyckoff accumulation).                                                                          
However, as a senior quant, I see several areas where the math needs more rigor to move from a "trading idea" to a 
"robust production system." To make this institutional-grade, we need to tighten the definitions of stationary vs. 
non-stationary data, replace qualitative thresholds with statistical probabilities (Z-scores), and refine the      
execution logic.                                                                                                   
Here is my critique and the improved specification:                                                                
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                      1. Universe Definition & Pre-Filtering                                       
Current: Basic liquidity and Beta filters. Quant Improvement: Introduce Market Impact Modeling and Sector          
Neutrality.                                                                                                        
 • Liquidity: Instead of just "Average Daily Volume," use a Participation Rate Filter. Ensure the average daily    
   volume is at least $10x$ the expected position size to minimize slippage in the execution model.                
 • Volatility (Beta): Beta $\in [0.8, 1.5]$ is too broad. Instead, target High-Beta/Low-Variance Convergence. You  
   want stocks with a high correlation to their sector but a low standard deviation of returns over the last 20    
   days (this identifies "tight" moves).                                                                           
 • Sector Weighting: Define liquidity relative to the sector_avg_volume. This ensures you aren't caught in an      
   illiquid niche that has high volume simply because it's a popular meme stock.                                   
2. Quantifying Consolidation (The Math)                                      
Current: ADX, Choppiness, and ATR Ratios. Quant Improvement: Use Standard Deviation of Price and Volatility        
Z-Scores.                                                                                                          
 • A. The "Quietness" Metric: Rather than just ADX (which is lagging), use the Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) of Log
   Returns. A consolidation zone is defined as a period where $\sigma_{short}$ (e.g., 15 days) is $< 0.5\sigma$ of 
   $\sigma_{long}$ (e.g., 250 days).                                                                               
 • B. Volatility Compression (The Squeeze):                                                                        
    • Instead of $ATR / Price$, use the Bollinger Band Width (BBW) as a percentage of mean price.                  
    • Define a "Squeeze" mathematically: $\frac{BBW_{current}}{Average(BBW, 100)} < 0.8$. This identifies instances
      where current volatility is in the bottom quartile of its historical distribution.                           
 • C. The "Box": Instead of "$\max - \min$," use Price Range Density. Calculate how much of the price movement     
   occurred in a narrow band relative to the ATR. If $Range_{10d} < 0.8 \times ATR$, it confirms a "flat" base.    
3. Validating via Volume Dynamics                                         
Current: Dry-up and VWAP proximity. Quant Improvement: Volume Variance and Distributional Analysis.                
 • A. Volume Dry-Up (The "Vacuum"): Quantify this using the Z-score of Relative Volume. You are looking for a $Z < 
   0$ on volume variance during the consolidation phase. This implies that retail and institutional "noise" is     
   subsiding.                                                                                                      
 • B. Volume Profile (HVN): Instead of just an HVN, measure Value Area. A high-quality base is one where price     
   resides within the $70%$ value area of the last 60 days' volume distribution.                                   
 • C. VWAP Deviation: The proximity to VWAP should be measured in Standard Deviations. You want Price $\pm 1\sigma$
   of the 30-day VWAP during the base period.                                                                      
4. Relative Strength (The Alpha Filter)                                      
Current: Simple ratio of Stock/Benchmark. Quant Improvement: Momentum Persistence and Selection Drift.             
 • RS Ranking: Instead of a raw calculation, use a Percentile Rank. $Rank(RS_{Stock}) >                            
   \text{percentile}(\text{Sector_RS}, 0.75)$.                                                                     
 • The "Anti-Correlation" Filter: The highest alpha is found in stocks that remain flat or rise while the          
   underlying sector/index is falling. This identifies Institutional Accumulation. Calculate the correlation       
   between $Price_{Stock}$ and $Index$ during the "Base" phase; you want a low correlation coefficient ($< 0.6$)   
   during the consolidation period to prove it is not just moving in lockstep with the market.                     
5. Multi-Factor Scoring (Systematic Rank)                                     
Current: Weighted linear sum of variables.* Quant Improvement: Rank-Based Composite Score.                         
Linear weights are dangerous because $ATR$ and $ADX$ are on different scales. Use a ranking system: $$CS =         
w_1(Rank_{Volatility}) + w_2(Rank_{RS}) + w_3(Rank_{Volume_Dry})$$                                                 
 • Thresholding: A "Trigger" is only valid if the stock ranks in the top 20% of its universe for at least 3 out of 
   4 metrics.                                                                                                      
6. Execution & Exit (The Trade)                                          
Current: Fixed breakout and ATR-based TP/SL.* Quant Improvement: Volatility Adjusted Stops.                        
 • Entry: The "Squeeze" is broken when $Price > \max(H, 20)$ AND $Volume > (\text{Mean Volume} + 1\sigma)$.        
 • Dynamic Stop Loss: Use a Chandelier Exit or an ATR-multiplier stop. Instead of a static level, use: $Stop =     
   Price - (k \times ATR)$, where $k$ is typically 1.5 to 2.0. This adjusts the stop based on the stock's "noise"  
   level.                                                                                                          
 • Time-Based Stop: If the price does not break out within $N$ days of the $CS$ score peaking, the trade is        
   discarded (stagnation risk).
